Chapter 3 — Audit Planning, Strategy & Execution

ICAI SM Final P3 May 2026 · ~8–10 marks Primary: SA 300 · SA 600 · SA 610 · SA 620 · Linkage: SA 315, SA 320 (Ch 4)

Scope correction to SPEC §11 map. The Ch 3 SM PDF gives full treatment (not refresher) to SA 600, SA 610 and SA 620 in §4.5.1 / §4.5.2 / §4.5.3 — these are primary-covered here, not in Ch 8. The chapter thus has four full-SA cards: SA 300 (planning) and SA 600/610/620 (execution-stage reliance on other-auditor / internal-audit / expert work). SA 315 and SA 320 remain Ch 4. SA 540 / 520 remain Ch 5, SA 450 remains Ch 4.

Chapter map

Three ideas run the chapter. Plan early, plan continually: planning is not a discrete phase but a continual & iterative process beginning right after the prior year's audit. Strategy vs plan: the overall audit strategy sets scope, timing, direction; the audit plan is more detailed and sets nature, timing, extent of risk-assessment & further audit procedures. Execution flows from the plan: execution = planning → risk & control evaluation → testing → reporting. SA 315 (risk) and SA 320 (materiality) are the two feeder SAs that parameterise the plan and are treated in depth in Ch 4.

Planning phase — timeline & linkages

SA 300 Planning an Audit of Financial Statements

Scope: Auditor's responsibility to plan an audit of FS — the context is a recurring audit; additional considerations for an initial audit engagement are separately identified. Core proposition: without a careful plan, the overall audit objective may not be achieved.

Objective / Core Requirement (verbatim from Ch 3 PDF)

"The auditor shall establish an overall audit strategy that sets the scope, timing and direction of the audit, and that guides the development of the audit plan."
The Ch 3 Final SM PDF narrates SA 300 through its requirements (strategy, plan, changes, documentation) but does not reproduce a separately labelled "Objective of the auditor" paragraph. The sentence above is the PDF's prose-list core requirement for SA 300 and is reproduced verbatim. No training-fill substitution was made.

Key Definitions & Distinctions

  • Overall audit strategy — sets scope, timing, direction; guides the plan; prepared first
  • Audit plan — more detailed than the strategy; sets nature, timing, extent of (a) risk-assessment procedures (SA 315), (b) further audit procedures at assertion level (SA 330), (c) other procedures required by SAs
  • Audit programme — operational instructions to assistants; list of procedures + time estimates; allocates work
  • Preliminary engagement activities — three activities undertaken at the beginning of the current engagement (see below)
  • Planning — continual & iterative — begins shortly after completion of prior audit; continues until completion of current audit

Benefits of Planning (PDF §1.1)

  • Attention to important areas
  • Timely resolution of potential problems
  • Proper organisation & management of the engagement
  • Proper selection of engagement team
  • Effective direction & supervision of team
  • Easy coordination with auditors of components & experts

Nature & Extent of Planning — drivers

  • Size & complexity of the auditee
  • Team's past experience & expertise
  • Change in circumstances during the engagement

Preliminary Engagement Activities — the mandatory three

At the beginning of the current audit engagement the auditor shall undertake:

  1. Procedures required by SA 220 regarding continuance of the client relationship & the specific engagement — integrity of owners/mgmt/TCWG; team competence & capabilities; compliance with ethics; significant matters from current/previous engagement
  2. Evaluate compliance with ethical requirements, including independence (SA 220)
  3. Establish an understanding of the terms of the engagement (SA 210)

Detailed treatment of SA 220 is in Ch 1; SA 210 is in Ch 2 — not repeated here per SPEC §10.

Overall Audit Strategy — factors while establishing (PDF §2.1)

  • Characteristics of the engagement — defines scope (FRF, industry reporting, component coverage, parent-component control, other auditors, service orgs, use of prior-audit evidence, IT, client personnel & data availability)
  • Reporting objectives — timetable, meetings with management, type/timing of reports, component-auditor communications, statutory/contractual reporting
  • Team's efforts — significant factors directing the engagement team's efforts
  • Results of preliminary engagement activities & knowledge from other engagements for the entity
  • Nature, timing & extent of resources — selection & assignment of team; budgeting

Benefits of the Strategy — it helps the auditor decide

  • Qualitative resources — experienced members on high-risk areas; experts on complex items (e.g. valuation, hedging)
  • Quantitative allocation — number of team members, extent of review of other auditors' work, budget hours to high-risk areas
  • Timing of deployment — interim vs year-end vs close-to-cut-off
  • Management of resources — briefings, partner & manager reviews, EQCR where applicable

Significant Factors & Preliminary Activities — matters to consider

  • Determination of materiality per SA 320
  • Preliminary identification of higher-risk areas for material misstatement
  • Impact of overall-FS-level RoMM on direction, supervision, review
  • Maintaining an inquisitive/questioning mind, professional skepticism, and unpredictability of procedures
  • Prior-audit deficiencies & corrective actions; discussion with other-service firm personnel
  • Management's commitment to sound internal controls; transaction volume; industry/economic/FRF developments

Contents of the Audit Plan — what the plan shall describe

  • N/T/E of planned risk assessment procedures (SA 315)
  • N/T/E of planned further audit procedures at the assertion level (SA 330)
  • Other planned procedures required for compliance with SAs
Planning the N/T/E of specific further audit procedures depends on the outcome of risk-assessment procedures — the auditor may begin executing further procedures for some balances/transactions before planning all remaining procedures. Sample size determination is part of "Extent" — must be decided in the plan, not at the moment of execution (TYU 2).

Changes to Planning Decisions — when must strategy & plan be revised?

  • Result of unexpected events
  • Changes in conditions (including law/notifications/policy changes)
  • Audit evidence obtained from results of procedures (e.g. substantive evidence contradicts tests-of-controls evidence)
Strategy change → plan change. When materiality is revised downward mid-audit because of newly-identified IC weakness, the strategy is modified first, then the plan (TYU-4 in PDF).

Discussion with management — watch-out

Elements of planning may be discussed with management for coordination. However, overall audit strategy & audit plan remain the auditor's responsibility. Discussing N/T/E of detailed procedures with management may compromise effectiveness by making procedures too predictable.

Involvement of Engagement Partner & Key Members

  • Engagement partner and key team members shall be involved in planning the audit — draws on experience, enhances effectiveness & efficiency

Documenting the Audit Plan — three items

  • The overall audit strategy — record of key decisions, communicates significant matters to the team
  • The audit plan — record of planned N/T/E of RAP & further audit procedures; reviewable & approvable prior to performance; standard programs/checklists may be tailored
  • Significant changes to strategy/plan & reasons — why changes were made; final strategy & plan adopted; appropriate response to events in the audit

Audit Programme — formulation considerations (PDF §3)

  • (a) Nature of business — visit client/factory on first appointment; understand processes, records, statistics
  • (b) Overall plan — programme adheres to plan; divergence from plan → modify plan first, then programme
  • (c) System of internal control & accounting procedures — review & preliminary evaluation, compliance testing, evaluation
  • (d) Size of organisation & management structure — branches, products, turnover affect complexity
  • (e) Information on organisation — history, nature, purpose of engagement, time schedule
  • (f) Accounting & management policies — review prior-year FS for consistency

First-time appointment — three stages

  1. Draw a broad outline of the programme
  2. After reviewing IC & accounting procedures, fill in details based on IC deficiencies
  3. After detailed checking, determine extent of special/opening-balance procedures — independent confirmation of debtors/creditors, physical inspection of FA, inventory counts

Circumstances requiring programme alteration

  • Substantial change in volume of turnover or in accounting organisation/procedures/personnel
  • IC procedures found less effective than assumed at design time
  • Extraordinary increase in book debts or inventory values vs prior year
  • Suspicion of misappropriation of assets discovered during audit

Conclusion / Reporting

Non-planning = violation of SA 300. Failure to understand the business & document the plan exposes the auditor to professional-standards non-compliance (PDF answer to TYU-1). The plan and programme are reconsidered as the audit progresses, informed by IC evaluation and compliance/substantive results.

SA 600 Using the Work of Another Auditor

Scope (PDF §4.5.1): establishes standards where the principal auditor uses the work of another auditor in respect of one or more components included in the financial information of the entity. Does not deal with joint auditors (SA 299) or predecessor-auditor relationship.

Objective / Purpose (verbatim from PDF §4.5.1)

"The purpose of SA 600, 'Using the Work of Another Auditor' is to: establish standards to be applied in situations where Principal auditor, uses the work of other auditor with respect to the financial information of one or more components included in the financial information of the entity."

Key Definitions

  • Principal auditor — auditor with responsibility for reporting on FS of an entity when that FS includes FS of one or more components audited by another auditor
  • Other auditor — auditor other than the principal auditor, with responsibility for reporting on FS of a component
  • Component — division, branch, subsidiary, joint venture, associated enterprise or other entity whose FS is included in the FS audited by the principal auditor

Acceptance as Principal Auditor — factors to consider

  • Materiality of the portion the principal auditor audits
  • Principal auditor's knowledge of the business of the components
  • Risk of material misstatement in FS of components audited by other auditors
  • Performance of additional procedures per SA 600 resulting in significant participation in such audit

Principal Auditor's Procedures

  • Right to visit & examine component books where statute confers — but ordinarily entitled to rely on other auditor unless special circumstances
  • Consider professional competence of other auditor if not an ICAI member
  • Obtain SAAE that other auditor's work is adequate; advise other auditor of use of his work, significant accounting / auditing / reporting requirements, time-table, areas requiring special consideration, inter-component transactions requiring disclosure
  • Discuss procedures with other auditor or review written summary (questionnaire/check-list) of procedures & findings
  • Consider significant findings of other auditor; apply supplementary procedures if deemed necessary
  • Document components audited by other auditors, procedures performed, conclusions; document how qualifications/adverse remarks in other auditor's report were dealt with

Exceptions / Conditions

Other auditor may be a person other than a professionally qualified auditor (e.g. foreign-country component where law permits) — principal auditor must form his own view of the work.
No duty to document reasons for limiting procedures where SAAE previously obtained that quality-control policies of other auditor's practice were complied with.

Reporting — division of responsibility

  • Limitation on scope — if other auditor's work cannot be used and principal auditor cannot perform additional procedures → qualified opinion / disclaimer
  • If other auditor issues a modified report — consider whether subject matter requires modification of principal auditor's report
  • Principal auditor not responsible for work entrusted to other auditor — except in circumstances that should have aroused suspicion about reliability
  • Report states division of responsibility — extent to which components' FS audited by other auditors are included in entity's FS
  • Statutory branch-auditor reliance (Companies Act) — auditor's report shall expressly state the fact of reliance

Principal–Component decision flow (Mermaid)

flowchart TD A[Entity FS includes financial info of component] --> B{Component audited by another auditor?} B -->|No — only me| Z[Regular single-auditor audit · SA 600 not invoked] B -->|Yes| C[Accept role of principal auditor — weigh materiality, RoMM, knowledge, additional procedures] C --> D{Other auditor an ICAI member?} D -->|Yes| F[Rely; advise on significant requirements & time-table] D -->|No| E[Assess professional competence in context of assignment] E --> F F --> G[Review written summary of procedures & findings · discuss significant findings] G --> H{Other auditor's report modified?} H -->|Yes| I[Consider whether to modify principal's report] H -->|No| J{Special circumstances requiring visit / additional procedures?} I --> K{Able to obtain SAAE on component?} J -->|No| L[Report states division of responsibility · extent of components audited by others] J -->|Yes| M[Principal auditor visits · performs additional procedures] M --> K K -->|Yes| L K -->|No — scope limitation| N[Qualified opinion or Disclaimer · SA 705]

Conclusion

Principal auditor retains opinion-level accountability; reliance on other auditor is structured — consider competence, coordinate, review, document. Report clearly indicates division of responsibility when basing opinion on others' reports.

SA 610 Using the Work of Internal Auditors

Scope (PDF §4.5.2): external auditor's responsibilities when (a) using the work of the internal audit function for audit evidence and (b) using internal auditors to provide direct assistance. Does not apply if entity has no internal audit function.

Objectives (verbatim from PDF §4.5.2)

"To determine whether the work of the internal audit function or direct assistance from internal auditors can be used; if using the work of the internal audit function, to determine whether that work is adequate for purposes of the audit; if using internal auditors to provide direct assistance, to appropriately direct, supervise and review their work."

Key Definitions

  • Internal audit function — function of an entity performing assurance & consulting activities designed to evaluate & improve governance, risk management and internal control
  • Direct assistance — use of internal auditors to perform audit procedures under the direction, supervision and review of the external auditor

External Auditor's Sole Responsibility

External auditor's sole responsibility for the audit opinion is not reduced by use of work of IA function or internal auditors giving direct assistance. Neither IA function nor internal auditors are independent of the entity as SA 200 requires of the external auditor.

Evaluating Whether IA Function's Work Can Be Used — three-pronged test

  • Extent to which IA function's organisational status & relevant policies/procedures support objectivity of internal auditors
  • Level of competence of IA function
  • Whether IA function applies a systematic and disciplined approach, including quality control
External auditor shall not use the IA function's work if any one of these three tests fails.

Nature and Extent of Work That Can Be Used — plan less use where:

  • More judgment is involved in planning / performing procedures or evaluating evidence
  • Higher assessed RoMM at assertion level — special consideration to significant risks
  • Lower organisational support for objectivity of internal auditors
  • Lower competence of IA function

External auditor must make all significant judgments in the engagement. Evaluate, in aggregate, whether planned use still leaves external auditor sufficiently involved given sole responsibility.

Communicate planned use of IA function's work to TCWG as part of SA 260 overview of scope & timing.

Using the Work — procedures

  • Discuss planned use with IA function; coordinate activities
  • Read IA reports of the work planned to be used
  • Evaluate whether IA work was properly planned, performed, supervised, reviewed, documented; sufficient evidence obtained; conclusions appropriate & consistent
  • Nature/extent of external auditor's own procedures responsive to judgment involved, RoMM, objectivity support & competence

Direct Assistance — conditions & limits

  • Evaluate threats to objectivity & competence of individual internal auditor — inquire of internal auditor regarding interests/relationships (family, financial, departmental association)
  • Shall not use internal auditor for direct assistance where significant threats to objectivity or lacks sufficient competence
  • Shall not assign procedures involving: significant judgment · higher assessed RoMM with more than limited judgment · matters reported by IA to management/TCWG · decisions the external auditor makes under SAs
  • External auditor shall direct, supervise and review IA-assistance work per SA 220; recognise internal auditors are not independent; review includes checking back to underlying audit evidence

Exception — law or regulation prohibiting direct assistance

Where law/regulation prohibits the external auditor from using internal auditors for direct assistance, the principal auditor should consider whether the prohibition extends to component auditors and, if so, address in communication to them (link to SA 600).

Conclusion

Framework prevents over-/undue use of IA work: use less of their work and do more directly as judgment & risk rise; responsibility for the opinion is undiluted.

SA 620 Using the Work of an Auditor's Expert

Scope (PDF §4.5.3): auditor's responsibilities regarding use of the work of an individual/organisation possessing expertise in a field other than accounting or auditing — valuations, actuarial liabilities, oil/gas reserves, complex tax, contract/law interpretation, complex FI valuation. Does not deal with: engagement-team member with accounting/auditing speciality; management's expert (SA 500).

Objectives (verbatim from PDF §4.5.3)

"(a) To determine whether to use the work of an auditor's expert; and (b) If using the work of an auditor's expert, to determine whether that work is adequate for the auditor's purposes."

Key Definitions

  • Auditor's expert — individual/organisation with expertise in a field other than accounting/auditing whose work assists the auditor in obtaining SAAE; may be internal (firm/network partner or staff) or external
  • Management's expert — expertise used by the entity in preparing FS (SA 500, not SA 620)

Auditor's Sole Responsibility

Auditor's sole responsibility for the opinion is not reduced by use of an expert's work. If, after applying SA 620, the auditor concludes the expert's work is adequate, the expert's findings/conclusions in that field may be accepted as appropriate audit evidence.

Determining the Need for an Expert

  • May be needed to: understand entity & environment · assess RoMM · design further procedures at assertion level · evaluate SAAE for opinion
  • Auditor not an expert in the field may still obtain sufficient understanding through experience, education/CPD, or discussion with peers — no auditor's expert needed in that case

N/T/E of Procedures — factors

  • Nature of the matter · RoMM · significance of expert's work in audit · prior experience with expert · firm QC coverage (esp. external experts)

Competence, Capabilities & Objectivity

  • Evaluate expert's competence, capabilities and objectivity for the auditor's purposes
  • For external experts: inquire regarding interests/relationships creating threats to objectivity; inquire of entity about known interests; discuss safeguards; consider written representation from external expert
  • Obtain understanding of the field of expertise sufficient to scope the work and evaluate its adequacy

Agreement in Writing — mandatory matters

  • Nature, scope & objectives of expert's work
  • Respective roles & responsibilities of auditor and expert
  • N/T/E of communication — including form of expert's report (formal written report for significant-risk matters; oral interim updates)
  • Expert to observe confidentiality — ethical requirements apply; additional legal/entity requirements may be imposed

Evaluating Adequacy of Expert's Work

  • Findings/conclusions — relevance, reasonableness, consistency with other audit evidence (inquiries, review of working papers, corroborative procedures, re-performance, discussion with another expert)
  • Assumptions & methods — generally accepted in the field, consistent with FRF, appropriate where specialised models used, consistent with management's (or reason for differences)
  • Source data — relevance, completeness, accuracy; verify origin, test IC over data, review for internal consistency

When Expert's Work Is Not Adequate

Agree on further work with the expert or perform further audit procedures (which may include engaging another expert). If matter cannot be resolved → modified opinion per SA 705 (insufficient SAAE).

Reference to Expert in the Auditor's Report

  • Do not refer to expert's work in an auditor's report with an unmodified opinion unless required by law/regulation (and then with a caveat that reference does not reduce auditor's responsibility)
  • Where reference is made to explain a modification → same caveat applies — responsibility for the opinion remains entirely with the auditor

Conclusion

Expert work is an evidence input, not an accountability transfer. Evaluate & document competence, objectivity, agreement terms and adequacy; modify opinion where inadequate.

Test-Your-Understanding takeaways (PDF)

  • TYU-1 (XWL · commodity-futures audit without a visit): blatant violation of SA 300 — failure to plan effectively and to document understanding of the business; generic checklist ≠ tailored plan.
  • TYU-2 (sample size decided during execution): improper — "extent" of procedures includes sample size and must be in the audit plan.
  • TYU-3 (FCRA society · extended foreign-contribution procedures, not documented): failure to document the plan & changes violates SA 300 documentation requirement; risk of non-qualitative audit.
  • TYU-4 (bank branch audit · errors missed by branch auditor): principal auditor not liable where no circumstance aroused suspicion; report must state division of responsibility naming branches audited by branch auditors (SA 600 linkage).
  • TYU-5 (niche-products listed co · inventory held long · IA silent on obsolescence): higher RoMM + significant judgment → less reliance on internal audit, external auditor performs direct procedures (SA 610 linkage).

5 Exam Triggers — spot these phrasings, invoke these SAs

  1. "Auditor in Delhi never visited the Surat office; generic checklist; no business understanding documented; sample size deferred to execution" → SA 300 — failure to plan effectively, document the strategy/plan, and fix extent (incl. sample size) at planning stage.
  2. "Mid-audit IC weakness surfaces; materiality to be lowered" → SA 300 — modify strategy first, then plan; document change & reason; link to SA 320 reassessment and SA 450 aggregate impact.
  3. "Principal auditor of a group: a large subsidiary's auditor issues a modified report on revenue recognition; principal auditor disagrees and refuses to modify his report" → SA 600 — principal auditor must consider whether subject of modification requires modification of his own report; if work of other auditor cannot be used and no additional procedures possible → qualified / disclaimer; report must state division of responsibility.
  4. "Listed co. has a well-functioning IA department; IA has valued closing inventory but missed obsolescence of long-held niche stock; engagement partner plans to rely" → SA 610 — higher judgment + higher RoMM → plan less reliance, perform more direct procedures; even so, sole responsibility is undiminished; communicate planned use to TCWG via SA 260.
  5. "Actuary engaged for gratuity valuation is the CFO's cousin; no written engagement terms; auditor plans to rely on the valuation in an unmodified opinion and cite the actuary in the report" → SA 620 — significant objectivity threat (external-expert relationship); no written agreement on nature/scope/roles/communications/confidentiality; no reference to expert in an unmodified report unless required by law (with caveat).
