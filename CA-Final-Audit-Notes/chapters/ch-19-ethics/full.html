Chapter map — how the ethics regime fits together

The examinable ethics universe has four layers. Every fact-pattern must be slotted into one of them. The CA Act 1949 sets membership, practice and discipline; the Schedules attached to it codify what is misconduct; the Council Guidelines 2008 operationalise limits and standards (tax audit 60, company audit 30, indebtedness ₹1 lakh, UDIN, logo, networking, corporate form); the Code of Ethics overlays the five fundamental principles plus the threats-and-safeguards framework and NOCLAR. The liability layer then pulls in the Companies Act 2013 (s.139 appointment, s.141 eligibility, s.143(12) fraud reporting, s.147 punishment, s.132 NFRA) plus civil/criminal law.

Case-study answer skeleton — the 5-step template

Every ethics fact-pattern must be answered in these five moves

  1. Provision — name the source with clause number: First Schedule · Part I · Clause (X) / Second Schedule · Part I · Clause (Y) / Council Guideline Chapter ___ / Code of Ethics · Subsection ___.
  2. Content — one line of the verbatim prohibition (quote the operative verb: "advertises", "accepts", "charges", "permits").
  3. Exception / carve-out — Council resolution, existing partner, regulatory body tender with minimum fee, UDIN exemption, statutory fee, etc.
  4. Analysis — apply facts to provision: is there a fee link, did communication happen, is turnover above threshold, is there a substantial interest, was Council permission obtained?
  5. Conclusion — guilty / not guilty of professional misconduct, and state the forum (Board of Discipline for First Schedule; Disciplinary Committee for Second Schedule) and likely penalty band.

Never skip step 1 — examiner awards marks only when the clause number is cited with the .clause tag. Never write "guilty" without saying of professional misconduct under [clause].

Code of Ethics — 5 Fundamental Principles (Subsections 111–115)

These five principles sit at the top of Part 1 of the Code of Ethics. They are not themselves enforceable as misconduct — breach is routed through the CA Act Schedules — but every threats-and-safeguards evaluation starts by asking which principle is at risk.

Principle · SubsectionCore duty (ICAI verbatim)Practical hook
Integrity · 111An accountant shall be "straightforward and honest in all professional and business relationships"; must not knowingly be associated with information that is materially false, misleading, negligently provided or that omits/obscures required information.Qualify or withdraw if associated with misleading FS.
Objectivity · 112"Not to compromise professional or business judgment because of bias, conflict of interest or undue influence of others." Do not undertake the activity if a circumstance unduly influences judgment.Decline if there is a financial stake or family tie.
Professional Competence & Due Care · 113Attain and maintain knowledge at the level required to give competent service, based on current technical/professional standards; act diligently, carefully, thoroughly and timely; supervise staff; be aware of limitations of services.CPE hours; refuse out-of-depth niche work.
Confidentiality · 114Respect confidentiality of information acquired in professional/employment relationships; do not disclose without proper and specific authority unless there is a legal/professional duty or right; do not use for personal or third-party advantage; duty continues after the relationship ends.Interplay with SS · Part I · Cl (1) (practice) and SS · Part II · Cl (2) (employment).
Professional Behaviour · 115Comply with relevant laws and regulations; avoid any conduct that "might discredit the profession"; no exaggerated claims, no disparaging references, no advertising in violation of Council guidelines.Social-media posts, logos, solicitation.
A professional accountant shall comply with each of the fundamental principles. The fundamental principles of ethics establish the standard of behaviour expected of a professional accountant.

Conflict between principles. Where complying with one principle conflicts with another, the Code requires the accountant to consider consulting with others within the firm, those charged with governance, the Institute, or legal counsel — and, if necessary, disassociate from the matter. The substance of the issue and rationale should be documented.

Threats & Safeguards — conceptual framework

The Code prescribes a three-step approach: Identify → Evaluate → Address. Evaluation uses the reasonable and informed third-party test — would another informed person likely reach the same conclusion? Threats must be reduced to an acceptable level; if not, eliminate the circumstance or end the engagement.

Safeguard examples from the Code

  • Assigning additional time and qualified personnel to required tasks (addresses self-interest).
  • Having an appropriate reviewer who was not a member of the team review the work (addresses self-review).
  • Using different partners and engagement teams with separate reporting lines for provision of non-assurance services to an assurance client (addresses self-review, advocacy or familiarity).
  • Involving another firm to perform or re-perform part of the engagement (addresses self-interest, self-review, advocacy, familiarity, intimidation).
  • Separating teams when dealing with confidential matters (addresses self-interest).

NOCLAR — Non-Compliance with Laws and Regulations

NOCLAR comprises "acts of omission or commission, intentional or unintentional, which are contrary to the prevailing laws or regulations" committed by the client/employer or those charged with governance, management or employees. It does not address personal misconduct unrelated to business activities.

Applicability in IndiaTrigger / Coverage
Section 260 — Accountants in serviceApplicable to Senior Professional Accountants (KMP-level directors / officers / senior employees able to exert significant influence) who are employees of listed entities.
Section 360 — Accountants in public practiceApplicable to audit engagements of entities whose shares are listed on a recognised Indian stock exchange and having net worth ≥ ₹250 crore. "Audit" means a reasonable-assurance engagement giving an opinion on true-and-fair view.

NOCLAR vs SA 250

  • SA 250 applies only to audits; NOCLAR applies to accountants in service and in practice.
  • SA 250 distinguishes direct-effect vs other laws; NOCLAR additionally covers non-compliance causing substantial harm (financial or non-financial).
  • NOCLAR defines stakeholders (investors, creditors, employees, general public) — SA 250 does not.
  • NOCLAR permits immediate disclosure to an appropriate authority to prevent/mitigate an imminent breach; SA 250 has no equivalent.
Confidentiality interplay. Disclosure under NOCLAR is read with SS · Part I · Cl (1) (practice) and SS · Part II · Cl (2) (employment) — disclosure is permitted where required by law for the time being in force, or otherwise with consent. Where disclosure would be contrary to law, IESBA precludes it.

Responding to NOCLAR — steps

  1. Obtain an understanding of the matter.
  2. Address the matter with management / TCWG.
  3. Seek advice.
  4. Determine whether further action is needed.
  5. Determine whether to disclose to an appropriate authority (e.g. SEBI for fraudulent FR).
  6. Handle imminent breach — disclose immediately if substantial harm to stakeholders is imminent.
  7. Document: management's response, judgments, decisions, how public-interest responsibility has been fulfilled.

Membership & Practice — CA Act 1949

Section 8 — Disabilities for membership

Name cannot be entered/borne on the Register if the person:

  • has not attained 21 years at the time of application;
  • is of unsound mind and so adjudged by a competent court;
  • is an undischarged insolvent;
  • being a discharged insolvent, has not obtained a certificate that insolvency was caused by misfortune without misconduct;
  • has been convicted of an offence involving moral turpitude punishable with imprisonment, or of an offence committed in professional capacity (pardon/quashing excepted);
  • has been removed from membership on disciplinary grounds (for the period of removal).

Section 19 — Particulars in the Register

Full name · date of birth · domicile · residential and professional address · date of entry · qualifications · whether holds COP · any other prescribed particulars.

Section 5 — Associate vs Fellow; Section 6 — Certificate of Practice

An Associate member can prefix "ACA"; after 5 continuous years in practice (or other prescribed qualification), an associate may be admitted as a Fellow ("FCA"). Only a member holding a Certificate of Practice (s.6) may be in practice; COP has to be renewed annually.

Section 25 — Companies not to engage in practice

No company, whether incorporated in India or elsewhere, shall practice as chartered accountants. Any director/officer knowingly a party to such contravention is punishable with fine.

Section 27 — Branch offices

Each branch office requires a separate member-in-charge resident at the branch. A member can be in-charge of an article's branch only if resident ≥ 182 days. A member in practice within 50 km of HO and engaged in the HO for ≥ 182 days may be deemed in-charge without a separate COP.

Section 24 — Penalty for falsely claiming to be a member

  • First conviction: fine which may extend to ₹1,000.
  • Subsequent conviction: imprisonment up to 6 months or fine up to ₹5,000, or both.

Disciplinary Procedure — s.21, 21A, 21B (Amendment Act 2006)

Section 21 makes a member liable to disciplinary action if he is guilty of professional or other misconduct defined in s.22 read with the Schedules or any other misconduct.

Advertisement Guidelines, 2008 — permissible write-up

A Write-up is permitted subject to these limits (breach → FS-I · Cl (6)/(7)):

  • Contents must be true to the knowledge and belief of the member/firm; no false, untrue, objectionable or unethical content.
  • No exaggerated claims; no disparaging references or unsubstantiated comparisons.
  • No testimonials or endorsements; no solicitation of clients.
  • No reference to fees, nor to ranking, size of firm, or list of clients.
  • Permissible content — firm's name & year of establishment, name & qualification of members, address, telephone/fax/email, services offered, areas of specialisation (generic terms), photograph, partners' CV.
  • Size, layout and medium limits prescribed by the Institute. Website must be on a "pull" basis (no push advertisements); compliant with the Council's website guidelines.
Social-media hook. Posts on LinkedIn/Twitter/Instagram announcing "empanelled auditor of X Bank" or "now auditing listed Y Ltd." cross into solicitation (FS-I · Cl (6)) and self-advertisement (FS-I · Cl (7)). Congratulatory posts by third parties that a member re-shares are also examined.

Liabilities of Auditors — civil · criminal · statutory

Civil liability

Arises out of negligence in the performance of duties — damages measured by the loss caused to the client / third-party user of FS who reasonably relied. Flows primarily from SS-I Cl (5) – (9) (non-disclosure, failure to report, due diligence, sufficient info, GAAP departure). Third-party liability (Candler → Caparo line) depends on proximity + foreseeability.

Criminal liability — IPC & special statutes

IPC 463/464 — forgery of a valuable security; IPC 477A — falsification of accounts; IPC 420 — cheating. Also PMLA, Income-tax s.277/278, SEBI Act s.24. Moral-turpitude conviction also triggers s.8 disability and FS-IV · Cl (1) or SS-III · Cl (1).

Liability under the CA Act, 1949

  • s.21 read with Schedules — professional/other misconduct (quasi-criminal): reprimand, removal of name, fine up to ₹1L (BoD) / ₹5L (DC).
  • s.24 — falsely claiming to be a member: ₹1,000 first conviction; ₹5,000 or 6 months or both on subsequent.
  • s.24A — penalty for using name/designation "CA" when not a member.
  • s.25 — companies practising as CAs: fine on directors/officers knowingly party (₹1,000 first; ₹5,000 subsequent).

Liability under the Companies Act, 2013

  • s.139 & 140 — appointment / removal / rotation: non-compliance attracts FS-I · Cl (9) for the auditor.
  • s.141 — eligibility / disqualifications: breach triggers SS-II · Cl (5) as well.
  • s.143(12) — auditor to report suspected fraud involving ≥ ₹1 crore to the Central Government within 60 days; less than ₹1 crore → to Audit Committee / Board.
  • s.147 — punishment for contravention of s.139–s.146. Fine on auditor of ₹25,000 – ₹5,00,000 or 4 times remuneration (whichever is less); if willful with intent to deceive — imprisonment up to 1 year and fine ₹50,000 – ₹25,00,000 or 8× remuneration. Firm-level joint & several civil and criminal liability on the erring partner.
  • s.245 — class action by members/depositors against auditor for wrongful acts.

Liability under s.132 — NFRA

NFRA has power to investigate professional/other misconduct of any member/firm auditing listed companies, large unlisted public companies, specified classes. Penalties under s.132(4): (a) fine — individual ₹1 lakh up to 5× fees; firm ₹5 lakh up to 10× fees; (b) debarment from audit/internal audit/any valuation for 6 months to 10 years. Concurrent jurisdiction with ICAI. Recent NFRA orders (Ch scenarios) illustrate findings on SA 230 documentation, SA 240 fraud risk and SA 700 modified opinion.

5 exam triggers specific to ethics

What the examiner plants in a fact-pattern — and the clause you must cite

  1. Fee accepted from the auditee as percentage / commission / contingent on findings → FS-I · Cl (10). Apply exception only for receiver / cooperative society / valuer / insolvency professional. Conclude: guilty, Board of Discipline, fine up to ₹1L.
  2. Tax-audit count crosses 60 per partner, or the firm's aggregate exceeds 600 → Council Guideline Chapter VI read with SS-II · Cl (1) (Council Guidelines breach). Remember: HO + branch = 1; part-time partners not counted.
  3. Member advertises on LinkedIn / Instagram / newspaper / radio or hosts congratulatory posts on empanelment → FS-I · Cl (6) solicitation + FS-I · Cl (7) advertisement. Test against Advertisement Guidelines 2008 limits — any ranking / fee / testimonial reference clinches guilt.
  4. Auditor accepts appointment without writing to the previous auditor (or signs before reply received, or uses unrecorded e-mail only) → FS-I · Cl (8). Communication must be by RPAD / recorded delivery with acknowledgement; polite reminder after reasonable time mandatory.
  5. Auditor is indebted to the auditee beyond ₹1,00,000 / has given guarantee, or company turnover ≥ ₹50 crore and non-audit fee exceeds statutory audit fee → Chapter X / Chapter IX + SS-II · Cl (1); parallel SS-II · Cl (5) if s.141 is breached. Progressive fee already earned is not "indebtedness".

Sanity coda

Every answer — at the Final level, in this chapter — must: (i) cite the clause number; (ii) quote the operative verb; (iii) identify the forum (BoD or DC); (iv) state the penalty band. Omitting any one loses marks even where the conclusion is right.
